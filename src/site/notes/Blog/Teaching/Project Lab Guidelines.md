A practical guide to working with me. 

---

## 1. Use AI, but own what it produces

You are encouraged to use AI tools for coding, writing, and exploration. That's the present and the future. But using AI without understanding its output is can be a trap.

If you show up to a meeting with code you generated an hour before and cannot explain what it does, that is a problem. The first time I notice this, you will not need to come to the next meeting; it is a clear signal that the project does not matter enough to you  in order to engage with it seriously. A project built on that foundation will not go anywhere. University should be about learning/understanding and not not about results in the first place, for that you'll have enough time after your studies.

Use AI to accelerate your understanding, not to bypass it. When you use a generated snippet, make sure you can answer: _What does this do? Why does it work? What happens if the input changes?_

---

## 2. KISS

Keep It Simple and Stupid. This is not an insult; it is a design philosophy and, in practice, the most reliable way to make progress without loosing motivation.

Begin with the most stripped-down version of your idea that still does something meaningful. No bells, no polish, no edge cases; the quick and dirty solution driven by desire rather than rational careful thinking. Get that working. Understand it completely. Then, and only then, add the next layer of complexity.

This approach makes debugging tractable, keeps your mental model accurate, and makes our conversations more productive. A half-finished complex system is almost always harder to reason about than a finished simple one. Build upward from solid ground.

---

## 3. Plans are optional, flexibility is not

You may make a project plan if it helps you think. I will not check it, hold you to it, or grade you on it.

There are two main categories of working cultures. One treats a plan as a contract: deviating from it feels like failure, and any significant change means essentially starting over. The other treats a plan as a starting hypothesis: useful at the outset, but subject to revision as results come in and the conversation evolves. I rather belong to the second camp.

This mode of working is genuinely messier. Directions change, priorities shift which can cause some frustration for some people who are less comfortable with uncertainty. I don't say either culture is better over the other, hence the existence of both. Yet, if you find it very frustrating, I am not the right advisor for you.

---

## 4. Topics can change

The aforementioned flexibility has a clear benefit: If the project direction does not feel right for you but you want to work with me, we can still change whole topic in the first few weeks of a semester.

The deadline for a topic change is the end of the first month of the semester under my supervision. The goal is for you to work on something you genuinely find interesting. Do not stay on a topic just because you think it is what I want.

---

## 5. Meeting notes are your responsibility

After each meeting, write a short set of notes and share them in our common folder as a single Markdown file, named by date if you like, or just kept as a running log.

The only thing I strictly need is (1) **the current status of the project**: project goals, assumptions, and main techniques as well as what have you done from the last meeting, and (2) a list of the next steps we agreed on. I supervise multiple students and will not reliably remember what we decided unless it is written down. 

Keep it brief. A few bullet points is enough. The point is alignment, not documentation for its own sake. For that, you will have the report at the end of project.

---

## 6. Changing advisor is always an option

At the end of any semester, you are free to move to a different advisor. No explanation is required, no hard feelings will follow. You are not obliged to continue any project.

If the collaboration is not working - the topic does not fit, the working style is wrong for you, or you simply want a different perspective - that is a legitimate reason to change course. I would rather you work well somewhere else than stay and be miserable here.

---

## 7. I do not debug your code

I have debugged enough code in my life. I have decided I am now too old for it, and I am happy with that decision.

More importantly: you will not learn anything without struggling through it yourself. The time you spend confused, trying things that do not work, and eventually figuring out why, that is where actual understanding is built. You will not appreciate this struggling while it is happening, but only later.

AI makes this easier than it used to be, and you should use it. But "easier" does not mean "free." You still need to understand the output and be able to explain it. The debugging skill that matters now is knowing how to work with AI tools effectively and that still requires you to engage, not just accept whatever is generated.

---

## 8. I ask a lot of questions

Asking questions is how I engage, and it is also how I learn. I am not testing you with every question; I am often curious myself.

That said, questions become uncomfortable when you come unprepared with code you cannot explain, or with nothing new to show, or with a vague sense that things are "going okay."  Expect to be asked _why_, _what happens if_, and _what did you try_, _what's the meaning of the formula that you (have) implemented_. Have answers ready, or be honest about what you do not yet know.

---

## 9. Independence is the goal

In English this course is called "Project Lab." In Hungarian it is _Önálló Labor_; independent lab work. The Hungarian framing is closer to what I am hoping for.

Ideally, I should be regarded as your compass, and not not your manager. I can point you in a direction, explain a paper you are stuck on, suggest when an approach seems unlikely to work. What I cannot do, and should not have to do, is provide your motivation from scratch. I don't want to be your source of motivation (in any sense).

Self-motivation here means arriving with a true interest in the topic before we even meet. For example: if you want to work on deepfake detection, I do not expect you to already know why detectors fail under distribution shift. But I do expect you to know why the problem matters: legally, technically, socially. You should have formed a view before asking me to help you refine it.

Many students find this harder than it seems at first glance due to many cultural reasons. In any case, be enthusiastic! It makes the work better for both of us.

---

## 10. Grading

I evaluate your (1) midterm work, (2) your final written report, and (3) your final presentation talk given during the supplementary week **together**. I will send you a short written evaluation at the end of that week.

I grade effort, not results. Negative results are also results! If you put serious work into a direction that finally did not work out, that is OK! What is not OK is to have some results built on shallow understanding. Make sure I can see the work behind what you show me during the meetings, in your report, and in your final talk.
